## WHY
Automatic memory management is one of Java's primary productivity drivers, sparing developers from manual memory allocation and deallocation (`malloc`/`free`), which are notorious sources of memory leaks, double-free vulnerabilities, and dangling pointer bugs. However, this abstraction is not free. Garbage Collection (GC) introduces runtime overhead, memory fragmentation, and—most critically—unpredictable latency spikes known as Stop-The-World (STW) pauses.

In modern enterprise applications operating at high throughput and low latency (e.g., e-commerce engines, real-time bidding platforms, microservices), poorly tuned GC mechanics can degrade system availability, cause request timeouts, and violate Service Level Agreements (SLAs). To build resilient, high-performance systems, developers cannot treat the JVM as a black box. A deep understanding of GC algorithms, generational hypotheses, memory layouts, and barrier mechanics is mandatory to select, configure, and tune the garbage collector to match the application's unique allocation and mutation profiles.

## THEORY

### The Weak Generational Hypothesis
Most garbage collectors are designed around the empirical observation known as the **Weak Generational Hypothesis**:
1. The vast majority of objects die shortly after allocation (e.g., local variables, short-lived transaction contexts).
2. The probability of an object surviving decreases exponentially with its age.
3. References from older objects to younger objects are rare.

This hypothesis dictates the division of the JVM Heap into two primary generations: the **Young Generation** (comprising Eden, Survivor Space S0, and Survivor Space S1) and the **Old (Tenured) Generation**. 

```
+--------------------------------------------------+-----------------------------+
|                     Young Gen                    |           Old Gen           |
+--------------------+--------------+--------------+-----------------------------+
|    Eden Space      |  Survivor S0 |  Survivor S1 |      Tenured Space          |
+--------------------+--------------+--------------+-----------------------------+
```
