## WHY
In a system design interview, if you propose an architecture that requires 500 terabytes of RAM to cache user profiles, you have failed the interview. The interviewer knows that 500TB of RAM is physically impossible or astronomically expensive. 

To design a system that works in the real world, you must prove that your architecture can physically handle the requested traffic using standard, affordable hardware. You do this through **Back-of-the-Envelope Estimation**—a series of quick, rough mathematical calculations to determine required storage, network bandwidth, and server capacity.

## THEORY
### The Numbers Every Engineer Must Know
To perform these calculations on the fly, you must memorize a few fundamental powers of 10 and data sizes:

**Data Sizes:**
- 1 Byte = 8 bits (A single ASCII character: 'a')
- 1 Kilobyte (KB) = 1,000 Bytes (A short paragraph of text)
- 1 Megabyte (MB) = 1,000 KB (A highly compressed photo)
- 1 Gigabyte (GB) = 1,000 MB (An hour of HD video)
- 1 Terabyte (TB) = 1,000 GB
- 1 Petabyte (PB) = 1,000 TB

**Time & Traffic Constraints:**
- 1 day = 86,400 seconds (Round to **100,000 seconds** for easy math).
- If a system has 1 million Daily Active Users (DAU), and each user makes 1 request per day, your system handles **10 Requests Per Second (RPS)**. *(1,000,000 / 100,000 = 10).*

## CODE

### Level 1 — Beginner (Estimating Traffic / RPS)
Always start by estimating the **Requests Per Second (RPS)**. This dictates how many web servers you will need.

**Scenario:** Design Twitter. We have 300 million Daily Active Users (DAU). On average, each user posts 2 tweets per day, and views 100 tweets per day.

**Write RPS (Posting Tweets):**
- Total Tweets per day = `300M DAU * 2 = 600 Million tweets/day`.
- Seconds in a day = `~100,000 seconds`.
- Write RPS = `600,000,000 / 100,000 = 6,000 Writes Per Second`.

**Read RPS (Viewing Tweets):**
- Total Views per day = `300M DAU * 100 = 30 Billion views/day`.
- Read RPS = `30,000,000,000 / 100,000 = 300,000 Reads Per Second`.

*Conclusion: The system is heavily Read-Biased (1:50 ratio). We will absolutely need massive caching layers to handle 300k reads/second!*

### Level 2 — Building Blocks (Estimating Storage)
Next, calculate how much hard drive space you need to buy to store this data for **5 years**.

**Storage per Tweet:**
- Text = 140 chars = 140 Bytes.
- Metadata (User ID, Timestamp, Location) = ~60 Bytes.
- Media: 20% of tweets have a photo (Average 1 MB).
- Average Tweet Size = `(80% * 200 Bytes) + (20% * 1 MB)` = `~200 KB per tweet`.

**Storage per Day:**
- 600 Million tweets/day * 200 KB = **120 Terabytes per day**.

**Storage for 5 Years:**
- 120 TB/day * 365 days * 5 years = **~220 Petabytes (PB)** of total storage required!

*Conclusion: We cannot store 220 Petabytes in a single SQL database. We MUST shard the database or use a distributed NoSQL blob store (like Amazon S3) for the images!*

### Level 3 — Intermediate (Estimating Bandwidth)
If you have massive amounts of data flowing into and out of your servers, you might saturate the physical network cables.

**Ingress (Incoming Traffic from Writes):**
- 6,000 Writes/sec * 200 KB = **1.2 GB per second**.

**Egress (Outgoing Traffic from Reads):**
- 300,000 Reads/sec * 200 KB = **60 GB per second**.

*Conclusion: 60 GB/sec of egress is massive and will cost millions of dollars in AWS bandwidth fees. We MUST use a Global CDN to offload the image downloads to edge servers!*

### Level 4 — Advanced (Peak Traffic and The "Rule of 2")
Traffic is never perfectly distributed evenly across all 86,400 seconds of the day. People sleep at night, and they check their phones during lunch. 

A standard industry heuristic is the **"80/20 Rule"**: 80% of the traffic occurs during 20% of the day.
To calculate **Peak RPS**, you simply take your Average RPS and multiply it by **2** (or sometimes 3, depending on the app's nature).

- Average Write RPS: 6,000
- **Peak Write RPS:** 12,000 

If you design your architecture to handle exactly 6,000 writes per second, it will crash every single day at lunchtime when the traffic spikes to 12,000. You must always design for the Peak.

## REAL_WORLD
Why does this matter? Because hardware has limits.
A standard, beefy modern web server (e.g., AWS `c5.2xlarge`) can handle roughly 10,000 concurrent connections, and roughly 1,000 to 3,000 HTTP Requests Per Second (RPS) depending on the framework (Node.js vs Go vs Rust). 

If you calculated your Peak Read RPS to be 300,000, and a single server can handle 3,000, you instantly know that you will need to horizontally scale to a cluster of exactly **100 Web Servers** behind a Load Balancer to keep the system online. 

## INTERVIEW
**Q: Do I need exact math in the interview?**
A: **Absolutely not.** The interviewer is not testing your ability to do long division. They are testing your mental models. You should round aggressively to make the math easy. Round 86,400 seconds to 100,000. Round 365 days to 400. If you try to calculate `(300,000,000 / 86400) * 365` in your head under pressure, you will freeze and fail the interview.

**Q: Should I memorize latency numbers?**
A: Yes! You should memorize the "Latency Numbers Every Programmer Should Know" (originally compiled by Peter Norvig):
- L1 Cache reference: 0.5 nanoseconds
- Main memory (RAM) reference: 100 ns
- Read 1 MB sequentially from SSD: 1 millisecond (ms)
- Read 1 MB sequentially from Magnetic Disk: 20 ms
- Send a packet from California to Europe and back: 150 ms

**Q: What if the interviewer doesn't give me the DAU numbers?**
A: You must ask them! "What is the expected DAU?" If they say "You decide," you pick a reasonable number and clearly state your assumption. "Let's assume this is a rapidly growing startup with 1 Million DAU to start." State your assumptions out loud before doing any math.
