## WHY
In the early days of the web, developers built layouts using `<table>` tags. Later, they shifted to building everything with `<div>` tags (resulting in "div soup": `<div class="header"><div class="nav">...</div></div>`). 

While this works visually, a `<div>` has absolutely no meaning. A search engine (like Googlebot) or a Screen Reader (used by blind users) looking at a `<div>` has no idea if it's the main article, a footer, or a sidebar. **Semantic HTML Elements** (`<article>`, `<nav>`, `<header>`) provide inherent meaning to the structure of your page, massively improving SEO and Accessibility without changing how the page looks.

## THEORY
### Semantic vs Non-Semantic
- **Non-semantic elements:** `<div>` and `<span>`. They tell you nothing about their content. They are just empty containers for styling.
- **Semantic elements:** `<form>`, `<table>`, `<article>`, `<nav>`, `<footer>`. They clearly define their content and purpose to the browser and assistive technologies.

## CODE

### Level 1 — Beginner (The Core Page Structure)
HTML5 introduced specific tags to replace the classic "div soup" layout.

```html
<!-- ❌ BAD: Non-Semantic (Div Soup) -->
<div class="header">
  <div class="navigation">...</div>
</div>
<div class="main-content">
  <div class="blog-post">...</div>
</div>
<div class="footer">...</div>

<!-- ✅ GOOD: Semantic HTML5 Structure -->
<header>
  <!-- <nav> is specifically for major navigation blocks (links) -->
  <nav>...</nav>
</header>

<main>
  <!-- <main> specifies the dominant content of the document. 
       There should only be ONE <main> tag per page! -->
  
  <!-- <article> represents a self-contained composition (like a blog post or news story) -->
  <article>...</article>
</main>

<footer>
  <!-- <footer> usually contains copyright data, author info, or secondary links -->
  <p>&copy; 2024 Dev Mastery</p>
</footer>
```

### Level 2 — Building Blocks (Sections, Asides, and Details)
```html
<main>
  <!-- <section> is a thematic grouping of content, usually with a heading. -->
  <section id="pricing">
    <h2>Pricing Plans</h2>
    <!-- content -->
  </section>
  
  <section id="testimonials">
    <h2>What our users say</h2>
    <!-- content -->
  </section>

  <!-- <aside> represents content tangentially related to the main content (a Sidebar!) -->
  <aside>
    <h3>Related Articles</h3>
    <ul>...</ul>
  </aside>
</main>

<!-- <details> and <summary> create a native Accordion/Dropdown widget! 
     No JavaScript required! -->
<details>
  <summary>What is your refund policy?</summary>
  <p>We offer a 30-day money back guarantee...</p>
</details>
```

### Level 3 — Intermediate (Text Semantics)
Semantic tags aren't just for massive layout blocks; they are also for wrapping text.

```html
<!-- ❌ BAD: Using CSS classes on generic spans -->
<p>He said <span class="bold">Wow</span>, that is <span class="italic">crazy</span>.</p>

<!-- ✅ GOOD: Using semantic text tags -->
<!-- <strong> implies high importance (Screen readers change their tone of voice!) -->
<!-- <em> (emphasis) implies stress emphasis -->
<p>He said <strong>Wow</strong>, that is <em>crazy</em>.</p>

<!-- <blockquote> is for long quotations from another source -->
<blockquote cite="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Jobs">
  Stay hungry, stay foolish.
</blockquote>

<!-- <time> represents a specific period in time -->
<p>The concert starts at <time datetime="20:00">8:00 PM</time>.</p>
```

### Level 4 — Advanced (Figure and Figcaption)
How do you semantically group an image with its caption? If you use a `<div>`, screen readers won't know they are related.

```html
<!-- The <figure> element represents self-contained content, like an illustration, 
     diagram, or photo. -->
<figure>
  <img src="architecture-diagram.png" alt="Cloud Architecture showing VPCs">
  
  <!-- The <figcaption> provides a caption for the <figure> element. -->
  <!-- Screen readers will read the image and immediately read this caption 
       as a grouped unit. -->
  <figcaption>Figure 1.1: Standard AWS VPC Architecture.</figcaption>
</figure>
```

## REAL_WORLD
**The SEO Impact**
Google's indexing algorithms rely heavily on semantic HTML to understand what is important on your page. If your main content is wrapped in `<main><article>`, Google knows exactly what to index for search results. If you wrap the same content in an `<aside>` (Sidebar), Google will intentionally de-prioritize it, assuming it is just tangential fluff.

**The "Skip to Main Content" Hack**
Screen reader users hate websites built with `<div>` soup because they have to listen to the screen reader read out all 50 navigation links in the header every single time they navigate to a new page.
When you use a `<main>` tag, modern screen readers provide a native shortcut allowing the blind user to instantly jump past the header and begin reading the actual article!

## INTERVIEW
**Q: Can I put a `<header>` tag inside an `<article>` tag?**
A: **Yes!** This is a common misconception. `<header>` does not mean "The top of the webpage". It means "A group of introductory or navigational aids". You can absolutely have a `<header>` inside an `<article>` (to hold the article's title, author, and publish date), and a completely separate `<header>` at the top of the `<body>` for the site navigation.

**Q: What is the difference between `<b>` and `<strong>`, or `<i>` and `<em>`?**
A: 
- `<b>` and `<i>` are **presentational**. They tell the browser "make this text bold/italic", but carry no semantic meaning. (Though HTML5 redefined them slightly, they are still considered mostly stylistic).
- `<strong>` and `<em>` are **semantic**. `<strong>` means "This text has strong importance". `<em>` means "This text has stress emphasis". Screen readers ignore `<b>` and `<i>`, but will audibly change the pronunciation/inflection of words wrapped in `<strong>` and `<em>`.
